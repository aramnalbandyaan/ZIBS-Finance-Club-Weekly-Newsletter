Ceasefire Talks Cool the Oil Shock — But the Economic Damage Is Already Done↗ OECD
Trump’s five-day pause on Iranian strikes and the emergence of a U.S. peace proposal sent oil crashing 11% in a single day — but with G20 inflation now forecast at 4.0% for 2026 and global growth under pressure, the conflict has left lasting marks on the global economy.

The biggest macro development of the week was not an economic data release — it was a Truth Social post. On Monday March 23, President Trump announced a five-day pause on U.S. strikes against Iranian energy infrastructure, citing “productive conversations” with Tehran about a “complete and total resolution” of the conflict. The announcement sent Brent crude crashing 10.92% to $99.94 in a single session — its largest daily drop since the war began and its first close below $100 since March 11. Oil markets had already priced in a prolonged disruption; the ceasefire signal unwound much of that premium in hours.

The relief, however, proved fragile. Iran formally rejected the U.S.’s 15-point peace proposal — delivered through Pakistani intermediaries — and laid out its own five-point counteroffer that included granting Tehran control over the Strait of Hormuz, a condition the U.S. called a “nonstarter.” By Thursday, Brent was back above $108, and Trump’s five-day pause on strikes was set to expire Saturday, raising the risk of a fresh military escalation into the weekend.

“If it persists, the conflict will weigh on global growth and push up inflation” — OECD Interim Economic Outlook, March 2026

The OECD published its March interim outlook this week, making its damage assessment official. ↗ OECD G20 headline inflation is now projected at 4.0% for 2026 — 1.2 percentage points above the prior forecast — before easing to 2.7% in 2027 as energy pressure fades. Global GDP growth is projected to hold at 2.9% in 2026, but that assumes the energy disruption is temporary and prices ease from mid-year. The five-year breakeven rate — what bond markets expect average inflation to be over five years — has risen 26 basis points since the conflict began, hitting its highest level since February 2025. Markets are now pricing in a 60% probability that the Fed will not cut rates at all in 2026, up from just 5.3% a month ago.

Consumer Pressure

Airfares have surged as oil prices hit airlines, which abandoned “fuel hedging” — the financial strategy of locking in future fuel prices — in prior years. U.S. farmers are also feeling the pinch: higher fuel and fertiliser costs are cutting into margins, testing Trump’s rural support base at a sensitive political moment. Wholesale prices rose 0.7% in February, far above expectations, with the annual rate reaching 3.4%.

Markets spent the week being whipsawed by ceasefire headlines. Monday opened with a dramatic relief rally after Trump’s Truth Social announcement: the Dow surged 631 points (+1.38%), the S&P 500 rose 1.15% to 6,581, and the Nasdaq gained 1.38%. All 11 S&P 500 sectors traded higher, with more than 90% of member stocks in the green. Oil crashed 10.92% in a single session, its largest daily drop since the war began, briefly closing below $100 for the first time since March 11. Industrial metals also bounced: copper rose 3% and palladium gained 1% as growth fears eased.

The optimism faded fast. Tuesday brought a 0.37% S&P decline as oil resumed its rally. Wednesday partially recovered (+0.54%) on news of the U.S. peace proposal. Then Thursday delivered the week’s gut punch: Iran formally rejected the 15-point plan, Brent surged 5.66% back to $108.01, and the S&P 500 fell 1.74% to 6,477. The Nasdaq — now down more than 10% from its high — entered correction territory. The Nasdaq’s drop of more than 10% from its peak is what markets technically define as a “correction.” The Dow shed 469 points. Treasury yields spiked on both the 10-year and 2-year bonds as inflation fears resurfaced.

The standout individual story of the week was Micron Technology’s stunning post-earnings reversal. Having closed at an all-time high on March 18 after its blowout Q2 results, Micron has since fallen 22% in six days — a dramatic sell-the-news reaction. Alphabet’s introduction of TurboQuant, an AI compression algorithm, spooked the entire memory chip sector, with Sandisk also down 20% in five days. BTIG analysts noted that Micron hadn’t fallen 20% in six days after a 52-week high since 1999 — and in that instance, it went on to fall a further 19%.

“We’re still living in a headline-driven market. With a light economic calendar, the focus will remain oil prices and politics.” — Chris Larkin, E-Trade

The week’s other notable moves: Arm Holdings surged after unveiling its first in-house CPU chip, earning an upgrade to “outperform” from Raymond James with a $166 price target. Braze jumped 19% after a revenue beat. Clear Secure surged 20%+ as TSA lines lengthened during the government shutdown. HSBC published a note suggesting Tesla could fall nearly 70% from current levels.

Who They Are

SpaceX is the world’s most valuable private company — a rocket manufacturer, satellite internet provider, and now artificial intelligence company, all rolled into one. Founded in 2002 by Elon Musk with the stated goal of making humanity multi-planetary, it has since built the world’s leading commercial launch business, capturing roughly 80% of the global rocket-launch market with its reusable Falcon 9 and Falcon Heavy vehicles.

The company’s most valuable asset today is Starlink: a constellation of over 7,000 low-Earth-orbit satellites that beams high-speed internet to 9.2 million subscribers worldwide. Starlink generated more than $10 billion in revenue in 2025, with analysts projecting $15.9–$24 billion in 2026. This subscription-based, high-margin business is what has transformed SpaceX from an aerospace contractor into a technology company capable of supporting a $1.75 trillion valuation.

In February 2026, SpaceX merged with Musk’s AI venture xAI in an all-stock deal, valuing the combined entity at $1.25 trillion. The merger integrates xAI’s large language model (LLM) capabilities — including the Grok AI assistant — with SpaceX’s orbital infrastructure, creating what is being called the world’s first “orbital intelligence” company. The SEC is scrutinising the deal’s disclosure to ensure the xAI valuation is fairly represented in the S-1.

The Bull Case
Dominant market position — SpaceX controls ~80% of global commercial rocket launches. No competitor — Boeing, ULA, Arianespace or China’s CNSA — has matched the cost economics of reusable rockets. The competitive moat is both technological and financial.
Starlink is a recurring revenue machine — 9.2 million subscribers paying monthly fees, with projections of $15.9–$24B in 2026 revenue. The business has software-like margins for a hardware-based product, making it highly attractive to public market investors.
Unprecedented retail access — If 30% of the IPO is allocated to retail, it gives millions of individual investors the chance to participate in what is historically a purely institutional event. That demand could create strong post-IPO buying pressure.
AI integration via xAI — The merger transforms SpaceX from a launch and connectivity company into an AI-powered infrastructure platform. The “orbital intelligence” narrative gives institutional investors a second reason to own the stock beyond aerospace.
The Bear Case
The “Musk discount” — Elon Musk’s simultaneous management of SpaceX, xAI, Tesla, and X creates significant key-man risk. Tesla’s share price has been under pressure partly due to Musk’s divided attention. Public market investors will demand a premium for that uncertainty.
Valuation demands perfection — At $1.75 trillion, SpaceX would immediately join the top 5 most valuable companies in the world. That valuation assumes Starlink revenue doubles, Starship succeeds commercially, and the xAI integration goes smoothly — a lot of things that need to go right simultaneously.
Regulatory scrutiny — The SEC is investigating the xAI merger’s disclosure. The FAA’s approval of up to 44 Starship launches per year is also being tested against environmental and orbital debris concerns. Regulatory friction could delay the timeline or require material changes to the S-1.
Market timing risk — The IPO window has narrowed significantly since the Iran conflict began. The S&P 500 is down 5.8% YTD and oil uncertainty persists. A June listing requires markets to stabilise between now and then — far from guaranteed.
Bottom Line for Finance Students

SpaceX is not a typical IPO. It’s not a startup seeking public market validation — it’s the world’s most dominant private company, going public to fund its next phase of expansion. The key concept to understand here is “narrative valuation”: at $1.75 trillion, much of the price reflects what SpaceX could become (a Mars colonisation company, an AI-in-orbit platform, a global internet monopoly) rather than what it earns today. That’s not necessarily wrong — Amazon was valued on narrative for years before the profits arrived. But it does mean the stock will be highly sensitive to any execution setback. Watch the Starship launch in April, the S-1 disclosures on Starlink margins, and the Fed’s rate path — all three will determine whether this IPO sets a record or becomes a cautionary tale.
