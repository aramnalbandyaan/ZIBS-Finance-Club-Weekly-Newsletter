Oil Shock Rattles Global Growth as Iran War Closes Strait of Hormuz
The U.S.–Israel military campaign against Iran has sent crude prices past $100 a barrel for the first time since 2022, stoking fears of 1970s-style stagflation just as the world economy was finding its footing.

The global economic outlook, already navigating tariff headwinds and elevated borrowing costs, now faces its sharpest supply-side shock since Russia's invasion of Ukraine. West Texas Intermediate crude surged 35% in a single week — the biggest weekly gain in futures trading history since 1983 — after Iran's newly appointed Supreme Leader declared the Strait of Hormuz a "tool to pressure the enemy," blocking a critical artery for roughly 20% of the world's seaborne oil.

The immediate macroeconomic effect is a stagflationary squeeze. Energy-importing economies across Asia-Pacific, Europe and North America face simultaneously higher input costs and softer consumer demand. University of Michigan consumer sentiment fell to 55.5 in March, with the expectations sub-index collapsing 4.4% in nine days.

"This conflict needs to end by end of the week. Otherwise, we'll see oil prices spike back above $100." — Asha Foss, Marex

The IEA moved to release 400 million barrels from emergency reserves — the largest coordinated action in its history — but markets remained sceptical. Goldman Sachs had forecast global growth of 2.8% for 2026, led by a resilient U.S. economy. That picture now dims considerably as inflation expectations re-anchor higher and the Federal Reserve is unlikely to ease until September at the earliest — a three-month delay from prior guidance.

Equity markets entered a third consecutive week of losses, with the S&P 500 posting a new 2026 low at 6,632 — 5% below its recent high. The selloff was uneven: energy stocks surged 2.5% for the week as Occidental Petroleum, EOG Resources and Shell hit 52-week highs, while information technology and communication services shed 1.1% each. Adobe led large-cap declines with a 7.6% drop after a guidance miss and surprise CEO departure.

The VIX spiked 24% to 29.49, a level last seen during the April 2025 tariff shock. Retail investors poured a record $211 million into oil ETFs on Thursday, with the United States Oil Fund (USO) seeing its third-biggest day of retail buying on record.

Cruise lines bore the brunt of the oil shock: Carnival fell 6%+ in a single session and is down 20% in March, while Norwegian Cruise Line posted seven consecutive negative sessions. Defence names outperformed — Lockheed Martin gained 6% and Northrop Grumman 5% after the scale of U.S.–Israeli strikes on Iranian infrastructure became clear.

"The situation is fluid and the risks are real — for now, we believe the shock is likely to be short-lived." — BlackRock Investment Strategists

Goldman Sachs pushed its first Fed cut expectation to September 2026, with a second cut possible in December — leaving the funds rate at 3.25–3.50% by year-end, well above market consensus.

What Netflix Gets

Netflix would acquire Warner Bros.' studios, HBO and HBO Max, and WBD's games division — including DC Universe, Harry Potter and Game of Thrones franchises. Co-CEO Ted Sarandos called it "combining two of the greatest storytelling companies in the world." WBD's linear cable networks (CNN, TNT, TBS, Food Network) will be spun off into "Discovery Global" before close.

Why It Matters

A Netflix–WBD combination reshapes global streaming, directly challenging Disney+, Apple TV+ and Amazon Prime with one of Hollywood's deepest content libraries.

The Paramount Risk

Paramount's offer is backed by $45.7B Ellison Trust equity and $54B in committed debt — a combined company carrying $90B+ in leverage that critics say could cripple content investment.

What To Watch

The March 20 shareholder vote is decisive. Regulatory clearance from the DOJ and European Commission follows if Netflix prevails, with deal close expected late 2026.
